How to set a boundary over text (without guilt)

The simple formula — plus 6 copy-and-adapt examples for family, dating, and exes.

Setting a boundary by text feels harder than it should. You write it, delete it, soften it, add three reasons why, and end up either not sending it or sending a paragraph that invites an argument. Here's the calmer way.

The formula: state it, keep it short, don't over-justify

A boundary is a statement, not a request for permission. The more you explain, the more you signal it's up for negotiation. Three parts:

  • Name the limit — what you will or won't do.
  • Keep it short — one or two sentences. Length reads as defensiveness.
  • Offer the alternative (optional) — what you can do, if there is one.

That's it. You don't owe a thesis. “No” is a complete sentence; a kind, short boundary is even better.

6 examples to adapt

Protecting your timeI'm not able to talk about this tonight. I can chat tomorrow after 6.
With a parent / familyI love you, and I'm not going to discuss my relationship choices. Let's talk about something else.
An ex who keeps reaching outI need some real distance to move on, so I'm not going to reply to these. Take care.
Co-parenting logisticsI'll keep our messages to scheduling and the kids. For anything else, email works better.
Someone pressuring you to decide nowI'm not going to make this decision tonight. I'll let you know by Friday.
Declining without an excuseThanks for thinking of me — I'm going to pass on this one. Hope it goes well.

About the guilt

If you feel guilty right after sending it, that's normal — and it usually means the boundary was needed, not wrong. The guilt is what makes you want to add “sorry” five times or a long justification. Resist that. A short, warm, firm message protects both the boundary and the relationship. You can be kind and still hold the line.
