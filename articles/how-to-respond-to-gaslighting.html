How to respond to gaslighting

When a text tries to rewrite what happened — why arguing the facts backfires, and 4 grounded replies.

“That never happened.” “You're imagining things.” “You're too sensitive.” Gaslighting works by making you doubt your own memory and perception until you're not sure what's real — and the natural response, proving you're right, is exactly what keeps you stuck.

What gaslighting is

It's the repeated denial of your reality to gain control of the narrative. The goal isn't to disagree about one event — it's to get you to distrust yourself so thoroughly that you defer to their version by default. Recognising it as a pattern, not a one-off argument, is the first step out.

The trap to avoid

Trying to win the factual argument. Long messages, screenshots, timelines — the more you fight to prove what happened, the more the conversation centers on your “need to be right,” which is the frame they want. You can't out-argue someone whose goal is to keep you arguing.

4 grounded replies

Trust your realityI remember it differently, and I trust my memory on this. I'm not going to debate whether it happened.
Decline the spiralWe clearly see this very differently. I'm not going to keep going in circles about it.
Name the patternThis keeps happening — I raise something, and it becomes me “imagining” or “overreacting.” I'm not okay with that.
DisengageI'm going to step away from this conversation. I know what I experienced.

Why these work

None of them try to “win.” They state your reality once, refuse the debate, and protect your footing. A practical tip: keep things in writing where you can, so you can rely on a record instead of your recall — that alone makes gaslighting much harder.

One important note. Occasional disagreement about memory is normal. But persistent gaslighting can be a form of emotional abuse. If a pattern is leaving you anxious, confused, or afraid, please talk to someone you trust or a professional — and if you ever feel unsafe, prioritise your safety over any reply. Quick answers

How do you respond to gaslighting over text?
Don't argue to win. State your reality and disengage: “I remember it differently, and I trust my memory. I'm not going to debate whether it happened.” Keep a written record where you can.
What does gaslighting look like in a text?
Repeated lines like “that never happened,” “you're imagining things,” “you're too sensitive,” or “I never said that,” used to make you doubt yourself.
