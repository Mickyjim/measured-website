How to respond when someone says “You always do this”

A calm read of what the phrase really means — and 4 replies that hold your ground without making it worse.

“You always do this.” Four words that make your stomach drop and your defenses shoot up. Before you fire back with “I do not always do this,” it helps to understand what's actually happening — because the obvious response is exactly the trap.

What “you always do this” really means

It's two things at once: a guilt hook and an exaggeration. The word “always” is almost never literal. It's an emotional generalization designed to make you defend yourself fast — and the moment you start arguing about whether it's “always” or “sometimes,” you've lost the actual conversation and handed it to them.

Underneath the accusation there's usually one real, specific thing that hurt. Your job isn't to win the word “always.” It's to find that one thing and talk about that.

The trap to avoid

The instinct is to defend: “That's not true,” “You always do it too,” or a long list of counter-examples. All three escalate, because they argue the generalization instead of the feeling. You end up debating word choice while the real issue gets buried.

4 calm replies that work

Pick the one that matches what you actually want — to repair, to slow things down, or to hold a boundary.

De-escalateI can tell you're really frustrated. I don't think it's every time, but I do want to understand what happened tonight specifically.
Hold your pointI hear that you're upset. I'm not going to agree it's “always,” but I'm happy to talk about this one thing if you tell me what it is.
RepairYou're clearly hurt and I don't want to brush past that. Can you tell me the moment that set this off? I'd rather fix the real thing than argue about “always.”
BoundaryI want to talk about this, but not while it's a list of everything I've ever done wrong. What's the one thing that's bothering you right now?

Why these work

Each one does the same three moves: it acknowledges the feeling (so the other person feels heard), it declines the exaggeration (without making that the fight), and it steers to one concrete point (so the conversation can actually go somewhere). That's the whole game — lower the heat, then stay with one thing.
